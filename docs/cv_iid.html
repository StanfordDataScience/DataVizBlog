Random splitting does not ensure true model performance¶

Be careful while splitting your data. -by Krishna Rao

First, a quiz. In the three examples below, think whether reported accuracy is fine.

  1. Sam wants to use satellite imagery to predict where forests are. Sam obtains some training data of images and human-labeled demarcations of forests. Sam then splits the images randomly into training, validation, and test set. After Sam is happy with his model, he reports his test accuracy as 99%!
  2. Janka gets a brilliant idea that she can predict heart rate just from IPhone movements. She collects time synchronized data from IPhone movements (features) and Apple watch (pulse rate, response) from thousands of consented users. She then splits the data randomly into training, validation, and test set. After Janka is happy with her model, she reports her test accuracy as a whopping 98%!
  3. Ask Avanti for an example

Why care about how we split data?¶

Splitting data into training, validation, and test folds, is one of the most standard ways to test model performance in supervised learning settings. Even before we get into the modeling (which gets almost all of the attention in ML), not caring about upstream processes like where is data coming from and how we split it can have bearing consequences on overall prediction. This is especially important when data has high autocorrelation. Autocorrelation is another way of saying value at a point is similar to values around it. Take temperature for instance. Temperature at any moment is expected to be similar to temperature in the previous minute (relative to temperature 4 hours ago). So we are going to have a model to predict temperature, we need to take special care in splitting the data. Specifically, we need to ensure that there is no data leakage between training, validation, and test sets so that we can correctly assess model performance, and improve on it.

nbins = 5
df["fold"] = pd.cut(df.x, bins = nbins, labels = range(nbins))

for fold in sorted(df.fold.unique()):
  train_index = df.loc[df.fold!=fold].index
  test_index = df.loc[df.fold==fold].index
  reg.fit(df.loc[train_index, "x"].values.reshape(-1, 1), df.loc[train_index, "y"])
  df.loc[test_index, "y_pred"] = reg.predict(df.loc[test_index, "x"].values.reshape(-1, 1))
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize = (5,5))

How to avoid splitting pitfalls?¶

  1. Always visualize your data before jumping into modeling

  3. Something else.

Conclusion¶

Splitting data can have huge consequences. If there is any evidence for data to be autocorrelation, stratified splitting, or other techniques for decorrelating the data using decomposition can be useful. So the next time you meet Sam, Janka, or Avanti. You are well equipped to help them come up with the best predictive model, without an exaggerated and inaccurate model statistics.
